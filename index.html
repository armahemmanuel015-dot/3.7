BLOCK 3.7 — SECTION B

MCQ Answers with the WHY and HOW — Questions 1–69
BURKITT LYMPHOMA CASE — Q1–5

1. Most likely histological/clinical variant?

✅ C. Endemic Burkitt lymphoma
Why? She is a 12-year-old child from Ghana, in sub-Saharan Africa. Endemic Burkitt lymphoma classically affects African children.

How? It is a mature B-cell lymphoma driven by MYC activation, usually from t(8;14), and is strongly associated with EBV in the endemic form.

2. Most likely head and neck presentation?

✅ B. Dental anarchy
Why? Endemic Burkitt lymphoma commonly involves the jaw and facial bones.

Tumour infiltration disrupts developing teeth, causing loose teeth, displaced teeth, premature eruption and abnormal arrangement of teeth — the classic “dental anarchy” appearance.

3. Best investigation to confirm the diagnosis?

✅ C. Core needle biopsy
Why? Lymphoma diagnosis requires tissue so the pathologist can assess morphology and immunophenotype.

CT/MRI can show the mass but do not establish histological diagnosis. Bone marrow aspiration is mainly useful for staging or detecting marrow involvement.

4. Cure rate for Burkitt lymphoma?

✅ D. About 90%

Burkitt lymphoma is extremely aggressive but also extremely chemosensitive. In appropriately treated children, cure rates can approach 80–90% or more depending on stage and treatment setting.

5. EBV implicated in what proportion of sporadic Burkitt lymphoma?

✅ A. 25% — best option

Endemic Burkitt has a very strong EBV association, whereas sporadic Burkitt has a much lower association. Among the options, 25% is the best answer.

DIC / OBSTETRIC BLEEDING CASE — Q6–10

6. Most likely diagnosis?

✅ C. Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)
Why? The major clue is bleeding from several unrelated sites at the same time — skin, oral mucosa and genital tract.
Massive coagulation activation → widespread microthrombi → consumption of platelets + clotting factors → severe generalized bleeding

7. Which clotting factors are commonly involved?

✅ E. All of the above

DIC is a consumption coagulopathy. Multiple factors are consumed, including Factor X, Factor II, Factor I (fibrinogen) and Factor VII.

8. Treatment of choice from the listed options?

✅ C. Fresh frozen plasma (FFP)

FFP replaces multiple clotting factors at once in an actively bleeding patient with DIC.

Remember: Cryoprecipitate is especially useful when fibrinogen is critically low; platelets are given for severe thrombocytopenia with bleeding; PRBCs replace red cells, not the full set of consumed clotting factors.

9. Which clotting factor is the primary cause of the current state?

✅ C. Factor VII — best available answer

The question is imperfect.

The true initiating molecule in obstetric DIC is Tissue Factor = Factor III. It complexes with Factor VII/VIIa and activates Factor X.

Tissue Factor (III) + VIIa → X → Xa → thrombin → fibrin → widespread coagulation → consumption → DIC

Because Factor III is not listed, Factor VII is the best available answer.

10. Ideal clotting test among the options?

✅ A. PT — Prothrombin Time

PT evaluates the extrinsic and common pathways, especially Factor VII, X, V, II and I. In real DIC workup, PT is interpreted together with aPTT, platelet count, fibrinogen and D-dimer/FDPs.

HAEMOPHILIA FAMILY CASE — Q11–17

11. Probability Mr Kofi would have haemophilia A?

✅ B. 1 = 100%, if the wording is taken literally

Hemophilia A is X-linked recessive. If his mother truly had hemophilia A (XʰXʰ), every son would inherit Xʰ from her and therefore be affected.

If the examiner actually intended the grandmother to be a carrier, the answer would instead be ½.

12. What percentage of Mr Kofi’s sisters had clinical disease?

✅ E. None of the above — essentially 0% under the usual assumption

Daughters receiving one affected X and one normal X are usually carriers rather than clinically affected, although lyonization can occasionally produce symptoms.

13. Percentage of Mr Kofi and Madam Hawa’s children who will express disease?

✅ A. 50%

Madam Hawa’s father had hemophilia, so she is an obligate carrier. If Mr Kofi is affected, the cross produces 2 affected children out of 4 possible genotypes = 50%.

14. Probability Edmund’s sons will have haemophilia?

✅ A. ½

Christiana is treated as a carrier. Each son gets Y from Edmund and one X from Christiana. She has a 50% chance of passing the affected X to each son.

15. Probability Edmund’s daughters will be normal?

✅ D. 1 = 100% clinically unaffected

Assuming Edmund is not hemophiliac, every daughter receives his normal X. Daughters can therefore be normal or carriers, but not classically affected.

Important: only 50% would be genetically non-carriers.

16. Common clinical presentation of hemophilia?

✅ D. Haemarthrosis

Coagulation-factor disorders cause deep bleeding — joints and muscles — rather than the petechiae and mucosal bleeding seen in platelet disorders.

17. How is Hemophilia B definitively diagnosed?

✅ C. Factor assay

Hemophilia B = Factor IX deficiency. aPTT may be prolonged, but confirmation is by Factor IX activity assay.

ANAEMIA / TRANSFUSION / PLATELETS — Q18–30

18. Colon adenocarcinoma blood film — finding that does NOT fit?

✅ D. Microcytic normochromic RBCs

Chronic occult GI blood loss causes iron deficiency, classically progressing to microcytic hypochromic cells with anisocytosis and poikilocytosis.

19. Blood product for serious warfarin overdose?

✅ C. Fresh frozen plasma

Warfarin reduces vitamin-K-dependent factors II, VII, IX and X. FFP replaces these clotting factors. In modern practice, life-threatening bleeding is usually reversed more rapidly with 4-factor PCC plus IV vitamin K where available.

20. 12-year-old with CHF and severe anaemia — best blood product?

✅ B. Packed red blood cells

PRBCs raise oxygen-carrying capacity while avoiding the extra plasma volume of whole blood — important in heart failure.

21. Classic contaminant able to grow in refrigerated stored blood?

✅ C. Yersinia enterocolitica

Yersinia can proliferate at refrigerator temperatures used for red-cell storage and can cause severe transfusion-associated sepsis.

22. Mild Hemophilia A treatment?

✅ C. Desmopressin acetate (DDAVP)

DDAVP increases endothelial release of vWF and Factor VIII, so it is useful in mild Hemophilia A.

23. Each random-donor platelet unit raises platelet count by?

✅ A. About 5,000–10,000/µL

24. Odd member among inherited predispositions to hematological malignancy?

✅ B. Klinefelter syndrome

Bloom syndrome, Fanconi anaemia, ataxia-telangiectasia and Wiskott-Aldrich are classic inherited disorders linked to hematologic malignancy risk. Klinefelter is the odd member here.

25. Normal CD4+:CD8+ ratio?

✅ A. Approximately 2:1

26. GI causes of nutritional/B12 deficiency — odd member?

✅ E. Tropical sprue — best exam answer

This question is imperfect because tropical sprue can also cause B12 deficiency. The other listed causes are more directly classic for B12 malabsorption.

27. Reduced mobilization of neutrophils from bone marrow?

✅ B. Lazy leukocyte syndrome

28. Immune-related cause of neutropenia?

✅ E. Felty syndrome

Classic triad: rheumatoid arthritis + splenomegaly + neutropenia.

29. Purpose of a major crossmatch?

✅ D. Detect antibodies against antigens on donor RBCs

Recipient serum/plasma is tested against donor red cells to detect incompatibility.

30. Forward blood grouping involves?

✅ A. Patient RBCs + known anti-A and anti-B antisera

Forward grouping identifies which ABO antigens are present on the patient’s red cells.

APLASTIC ANAEMIA / SICKLE CELL / B12 — Q31–46

31. Virus implicated in aplastic anaemia?

✅ B. HBV — best answer

Hepatitis-associated aplastic anaemia is a recognized syndrome. Parvovirus B19 classically causes pure red cell aplasia or transient aplastic crisis rather than generalized pancytopenic aplastic anaemia.

32. Sudden pooling of RBCs in spleen in sickle cell disease?

✅ B. Sequestration crisis
Splenic trapping → rapid splenic enlargement → acute fall in circulating blood volume → severe anaemia ± shock

33. Organ/tissue commonly affected in vitamin B12 deficiency?

✅ B. Gastrointestinal mucosa

Rapidly dividing GI epithelial cells are strongly affected because B12 deficiency impairs DNA synthesis. Neurological tissue is also importantly affected.

34. Neural tube defects associated with deficiency of?

✅ D. Folate

35. Which genotype defines sickle disease?

✅ C. Genotype SS — likely intended answer

Technically, sickle cell disease also includes HbSC and HbS/β-thalassemia. HbSS specifically defines classic sickle cell anaemia.

36. HbA₂ concentration in a neonate?

✅ C. About 0.5%

Delta-chain production is low at birth, so HbA₂ is very low in neonates.

37. Transport protein synthesized in stomach?

✅ B. Intrinsic factor

Produced by gastric parietal cells and required for B12 absorption in the terminal ileum.

38. Parasite associated with iron-deficiency anaemia?

✅ C. Schistosoma haematobium — best option given

The classic parasite causing iron deficiency is hookworm, but it is not listed. S. haematobium can cause chronic urinary blood loss and iron deficiency.

39. Intestinal causes of B12 deficiency EXCEPT?

✅ C. Pernicious anaemia

Pernicious anaemia is primarily an autoimmune gastric disorder causing intrinsic-factor deficiency.

40. Where is B12 absorbed?

✅ B. Ileum — specifically terminal ileum

41. First protein that binds food-derived B12?

✅ D. Cobalophilins / Haptocorrin
Food B12 → haptocorrin first → released in duodenum → intrinsic factor → terminal ileum absorption

42. Oral iron absorption is maximal where?

✅ C. First part of the duodenum

43. Percentage of oral iron absorbed?

✅ A. About 5–10%

44. RBC index used for morphological classification of anaemia?

✅ D. MCV

Low MCV = microcytic; normal MCV = normocytic; high MCV = macrocytic.

45. First site of fetal hematopoiesis?

✅ C. Yolk sac
Yolk sac → liver → spleen contribution → bone marrow

46. Hormone regulating/sensing need for erythropoiesis?

✅ C. Erythropoietin
Low oxygen → renal HIF activation → ↑ EPO → marrow erythroid proliferation → ↑ RBCs
HAEMATOPOIESIS / PLATELETS — Q47–53

47. Cofactors for erythropoiesis EXCEPT?

✅ E. Calcium

48. Commonest haemoglobin in cord blood?

✅ C. α₂γ₂ = HbF

49. Rate of haemopoiesis in a healthy adult?

✅ C. Approximately 10¹² new blood cells/day

50. Percentage of normal adult haemopoietic activity occurring in bone marrow?

✅ E. 100% — intended answer

51. Growth factor acting on very early/pluripotent stem cells?

✅ C. SCF — Stem Cell Factor

SCF acts through c-KIT and supports survival and proliferation of early hematopoietic stem/progenitor cells.

52. Weibel-Palade bodies are synthesized in?

✅ D. Endothelial cells

They store von Willebrand factor and P-selectin.

53. Normal platelet lifespan?

✅ C. About 9–10 days
FIGURE 1 — LIKELY MULTIPLE MYELOMA — Q54–57

54. Renal pathology that may result?

✅ A. Amyloidosis — best pathological answer

Myeloma light chains can cause cast nephropathy, AL amyloidosis and light-chain deposition disease. Since cast nephropathy is not listed, amyloidosis is the best option.

55. Likely clinical feature?

✅ E. Pathological fracture

Osteolytic lesions weaken bone, producing bone pain and pathological fractures.

56. Condition is more common in?

✅ E. Elderly Black women — best available option

Multiple myeloma predominantly affects older adults and is more common in people of African ancestry. Men are actually somewhat more commonly affected than women, so the option is not perfect.

57. What happens to ESR?

✅ D. ESR will be high

Monoclonal immunoglobulins promote rouleaux formation, making red cells sediment faster.

FIGURE 2 — HODGKIN LYMPHOMA — Q58–61

58. Right supraclavicular + right para-aortic nodes = what Ann Arbor stage?

✅ B. Stage III

Nodal disease exists above and below the diaphragm.

59. Most likely anaemia?

✅ A. Normochromic — usually normocytic normochromic

Chronic inflammation increases hepcidin, trapping iron and reducing erythropoiesis.

60. Which immunity is impaired?

✅ C. Cellular immunity

Hodgkin lymphoma is classically associated with impaired T-cell-mediated immunity.

61. Risk of which malignancy?

✅ A. Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma — best option

Patients with Hodgkin lymphoma, especially after treatment, have increased risk of secondary malignancies including non-Hodgkin lymphomas.

FIGURE 3 — ACUTE LEUKAEMIA — Q62–66

62. FAB classification?

✅ A. ALL L2

Old FAB L2 morphology describes larger, more heterogeneous lymphoblasts than L1.

63. Which age group is L2 historically more common in?

✅ C. Adults

ALL overall is commonest in children, but FAB L2 morphology was historically relatively more associated with adults.

64. Common cell lineage?

✅ A. B lymphocytes

Most childhood ALL is B-lymphoblastic leukemia/lymphoma.

65. Prognosis?

✅ C. Bad — in the old FAB framework

Historically L2 morphology had worse prognosis than L1. Modern prognosis depends much more on genetics, age, WBC count, treatment response and measurable residual disease.

66. Common secondary/sanctuary site?

✅ E. Brain/CNS

The CNS is a major sanctuary site in ALL, which is why CNS-directed prophylaxis is part of therapy.

FIGURE 4 — PREGNANT WOMAN / VERY HIGH PLATELET COUNT — Q67–69

67. Most likely haematological abnormality?

✅ B. Thrombophilia — intended answer

Technically, the actual abnormality is thrombocytosis. “Thrombophilia” means a tendency to thrombosis.

68. She may present with?

✅ B. Thrombosis

Marked thrombocytosis can increase risk of arterial, venous and microvascular thrombosis.

69. Most appropriate test from the listed options?

✅ C. Bleeding time — intended older exam answer

PT and aPTT assess coagulation pathways, whereas bleeding time was historically used to assess primary haemostasis/platelet function. Modern practice relies on more specific platelet and vWF testing.

5 HIGH-YIELD THINGS NOT TO GET TRICKED BY
DIC: Tissue Factor III is the true initiator → Factor VII → X → thrombin. If Factor III is absent from the options, VII may be the best available answer.
Hemophilia: Deep bleeding and haemarthrosis, not petechiae. Hemophilia A = Factor VIII deficiency; Hemophilia B = Factor IX deficiency. Confirm by factor assay.
B12 journey: Haptocorrin/cobalophilin first → intrinsic factor later → terminal ileum absorption.
Iron + anaemia classification: Iron is absorbed mainly in the duodenum. Morphological classification of anaemia is based primarily on MCV.
ALL: ALL overall is mainly a childhood disease, but old FAB L2 morphology was relatively more associated with adults.
