Burkitt lymphoma case — Q1–5

Question 1

Which histological/clinical variant of Burkitt lymphoma is most likely in this 12-year-old child from Nandom with a rapidly growing mass?

  1. A. Organ-transplant related
  2. B. Diffuse B-cell lymphoma / HIV-associated variant
  3. C. Endemic
  4. D. Chemotherapy-related
  5. E. Sporadic
AnswerC. Endemic Burkitt lymphoma
Why / how: Endemic Burkitt lymphoma classically affects children in equatorial/sub-Saharan Africa. The important mechanism is MYC activation, usually by t(8;14), causing extremely rapid B-cell proliferation. EBV association is very strong in the endemic form.
Question 2

What is the most likely oral/head-and-neck presentation of endemic Burkitt lymphoma?

  1. A. Bleeding gums
  2. B. Dental anarchy
  3. C. Ulcers
  4. D. Dental cysts
  5. E. Cervical lymphadenopathy
AnswerB. Dental anarchy
Why / how: Endemic Burkitt lymphoma often involves the jaw and facial bones. Tumour infiltration disturbs developing teeth, producing loose, displaced or prematurely erupted teeth — the classic 'dental anarchy' appearance.
Question 3

What is the best investigation to confirm the diagnosis?

  1. A. CT scan of the head
  2. B. MRI of the head
  3. C. Core needle biopsy
  4. D. Bone marrow aspirate
  5. E. X-ray of the skull
AnswerC. Core needle biopsy
Why / how: Lymphoma is a tissue diagnosis. Imaging shows the mass but does not establish histology. A tissue biopsy allows morphology, immunophenotyping and further classification. Bone marrow examination is mainly useful for staging.
Question 4

What is the approximate cure rate for Burkitt lymphoma with appropriate treatment?

  1. A. 60%
  2. B. 67%
  3. C. 75%
  4. D. About 90%
  5. E. 35%
AnswerD. About 90%
Why / how: Burkitt lymphoma grows extremely fast but is also highly chemosensitive. Cure rates in appropriately treated children can approach 80–90% or higher depending on stage and resources.
Question 5

EBV is implicated in approximately what proportion of sporadic Burkitt lymphoma?

  1. A. 25%
  2. B. 30%
  3. C. 45%
  4. D. 35%
  5. E. 90%
AnswerA. 25% — best option
Why / how: EBV is present in most endemic Burkitt lymphomas, but only a minority of sporadic cases. Among the listed choices, 25% is the best fit.

DIC / obstetric bleeding case — Q6–10

Question 6

A 14-year-old adolescent female has 2 months of amenorrhoea, severe lower abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, and bleeding from the skin, vulva, cervix and oral mucosa. What is the most likely diagnosis at presentation?

  1. A. Haemolytic disease of the newborn
  2. B. Haemolytic anaemia
  3. C. Disseminated intravascular coagulation
  4. D. Placental abruption
  5. E. Haemorrhagic shock
AnswerC. Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)
Why / how: Bleeding from multiple unrelated sites strongly suggests a systemic consumption coagulopathy. In DIC, coagulation is activated widely, microthrombi form, and platelets plus clotting factors are consumed. The patient then bleeds because the haemostatic system has been exhausted.
Question 7

Which clotting factors are commonly involved/consumed in DIC?

  1. A. Factor X
  2. B. Factor II
  3. C. Factor I
  4. D. Factor VII
  5. E. All of the above
AnswerE. All of the above
Why / how: DIC consumes multiple clotting factors and platelets. Factor I is fibrinogen, which may become markedly reduced; Factors II, VII and X are also consumed.
Question 8

What is the treatment of choice from the listed blood products in this actively bleeding patient with DIC?

  1. A. Packed red blood cells
  2. B. Cryoprecipitate
  3. C. Fresh frozen plasma
  4. D. Whole blood
  5. E. Platelet concentrate
AnswerC. Fresh frozen plasma (FFP)
Why / how: FFP replaces multiple coagulation factors at once. Cryoprecipitate is particularly useful when fibrinogen is critically low, platelets are used when thrombocytopenia is severe with bleeding, and PRBCs replace red cells rather than the full set of consumed factors.
Question 9

Which clotting factor is the best available answer for the factor primarily involved in initiating this patient's DIC?

  1. A. Factor XII
  2. B. Factor X
  3. C. Factor VII
  4. D. Calcium
  5. E. Factor II
AnswerC. Factor VII — best available answer
Why / how: The true initiator in obstetric DIC is tissue factor (Factor III), but it is not listed. Tissue factor binds Factor VII/VIIa, which activates Factor X, leading to a thrombin burst, widespread fibrin formation, consumption of platelets/factors, and DIC.
Question 10

Which clotting test is the best single test from the options to request in this pathway?

  1. A. PT
  2. B. vWF factor assay
  3. C. PTT
  4. D. Bedside bleeding time
  5. E. None of the above
AnswerA. PT — Prothrombin Time
Why / how: PT assesses the extrinsic and common pathways, especially Factor VII and downstream Factors X, V, II and I. In real DIC evaluation, PT is interpreted with aPTT, platelet count, fibrinogen and D-dimer/FDPs.

Haemophilia family case — Q11–17

Question 11

Given the family history in the stem, what is the probability that Mr Kofi would have haemophilia A?

  1. A. 1/2
  2. B. 1
  3. C. 1/4
  4. D. 1/8
  5. E. 1/3
AnswerB. 1 = 100% if the wording is taken literally
Why / how: Haemophilia A is X-linked recessive. If Mr Kofi's mother truly had haemophilia A (XʰXʰ), every son would receive Xʰ from her and therefore be affected. If the examiner merely intended her to be a carrier, the probability would instead be 1/2.
Question 12

What percentage of Mr Kofi's sisters would have clinical haemophilia under the usual inheritance assumption?

  1. A. 50%
  2. B. 25%
  3. C. 75%
  4. D. 100%
  5. E. None of the above
AnswerE. None of the above — essentially 0% in the usual scenario
Why / how: Female relatives who inherit one abnormal X and one normal X are usually carriers, not classically affected. Symptomatic females can occur because of skewed X-inactivation, but that is not the standard exam assumption.
Question 13

What percentage of the children of Mr Kofi and Madam Hawa would be expected to express haemophilia in the pedigree described?

  1. A. 50%
  2. B. 25%
  3. C. 75%
  4. D. 100%
  5. E. None of the above
AnswerA. 50%
Why / how: Madam Hawa's father had haemophilia, so she is an obligate carrier. If Mr Kofi is affected, the cross can produce affected sons and affected daughters; two of four basic genotype outcomes are affected, giving 50%.
Question 14

What is the probability that Edmund's sons would have haemophilia, assuming Christiana is a carrier?

  1. A. 1/2
  2. B. 1/4
  3. C. 1/8
  4. D. None of the above
  5. E. 1
AnswerA. 1/2
Why / how: A son receives Y from his father and his only X chromosome from his mother. A carrier mother has a 50% chance of transmitting the affected X to each son.
Question 15

What is the probability that Edmund's daughters will be clinically normal, assuming Edmund does not have haemophilia?

  1. A. 1/2
  2. B. 1/4
  3. C. 2/3
  4. D. 1
  5. E. None of the above
AnswerD. 1 = 100% clinically unaffected
Why / how: Every daughter receives Edmund's normal X. Therefore daughters may be genetically normal or carriers, but they would not have classical haemophilia. Only 50% would be completely non-carriers.
Question 16

What is the common clinical presentation of haemophilia?

  1. A. Prolonged bleeding time
  2. B. Petechial haemorrhages
  3. C. Bleeding gums
  4. D. Haemarthrosis
  5. E. Anaemia
AnswerD. Haemarthrosis
Why / how: Coagulation-factor deficiencies cause deep-tissue bleeding, especially bleeding into joints and muscles. Petechiae and mucosal bleeding point more toward platelet disorders.
Question 17

How is haemophilia B definitively diagnosed?

  1. A. PTT
  2. B. PT
  3. C. Factor assay
  4. D. Clinical history
  5. E. D-dimer assay
AnswerC. Factor assay
Why / how: Haemophilia B is Factor IX deficiency. aPTT may be prolonged, but definitive diagnosis is by measuring Factor IX activity.

Anaemia, transfusion & platelets — Q18–30

Question 18

A peripheral blood film from an elderly woman with adenocarcinoma of the colon will show all of the following EXCEPT which finding?

  1. A. Anisocytosis
  2. B. Poikilocytosis
  3. C. Normocytic normochromic cells may occur early
  4. D. Microcytic normochromic red cells
  5. E. Microcytic hypochromic red cells
AnswerD. Microcytic normochromic red cells
Why / how: Chronic occult GI blood loss causes iron deficiency. The classic later picture is microcytic hypochromic anaemia with anisocytosis and poikilocytosis. 'Microcytic normochromic' is the least fitting combination.
Question 19

Which blood product is used for urgent replacement of vitamin-K-dependent clotting factors in serious warfarin-associated bleeding, from the listed options?

  1. A. Whole blood
  2. B. Platelet concentrate
  3. C. Fresh frozen plasma
  4. D. Packed red cells
  5. E. Cryoprecipitate
AnswerC. Fresh frozen plasma
Why / how: Warfarin reduces Factors II, VII, IX and X. FFP contains these factors. Modern emergency reversal generally prefers 4-factor PCC plus IV vitamin K when available, but PCC is not among the listed options.
Question 20

A 12-year-old with congestive heart failure due to severe anaemia needs transfusion. Which blood product is most appropriate?

  1. A. Whole blood
  2. B. Packed red blood cells
  3. C. Fresh frozen plasma
  4. D. Platelet concentrate
  5. E. Cryoprecipitate
AnswerB. Packed red blood cells
Why / how: PRBCs raise oxygen-carrying capacity with less unnecessary plasma volume than whole blood, which is especially important in heart failure where volume overload can worsen the condition.
Question 21

Which organism is a classic bacterial contaminant capable of proliferating in refrigerated stored red-cell units?

  1. A. Staphylococcus aureus
  2. B. Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  3. C. Yersinia enterocolitica
  4. D. E. coli
  5. E. Salmonella
AnswerC. Yersinia enterocolitica
Why / how: Yersinia can grow at refrigerator temperatures, making it a classic cause of severe transfusion-associated sepsis from stored red cells.
Question 22

Mild haemophilia A can be treated with which of the following?

  1. A. Whole blood
  2. B. Packed red cells
  3. C. Desmopressin acetate
  4. D. Platelet concentrate
  5. E. Aspirin
AnswerC. Desmopressin acetate (DDAVP)
Why / how: DDAVP releases stored von Willebrand factor and increases circulating Factor VIII, making it useful in mild haemophilia A. It does not treat haemophilia B.
Question 23

Each unit of random-donor platelets typically raises the platelet count by approximately how much?

  1. A. 5,000–10,000/µL
  2. B. 2,000–3,000/µL
  3. C. 2,500–4,000/µL
  4. D. 500–1,000/µL
  5. E. 20–700/µL
AnswerA. About 5,000–10,000/µL
Why / how: A single random-donor platelet unit produces a relatively small increment. A full adult therapeutic dose or apheresis unit produces a larger rise.
Question 24

Which is the odd member among classic inherited disorders that predispose to haematological malignancy?

  1. A. Bloom syndrome
  2. B. Klinefelter syndrome
  3. C. Fanconi anaemia
  4. D. Ataxia-telangiectasia
  5. E. Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome
AnswerB. Klinefelter syndrome
Why / how: Bloom syndrome, Fanconi anaemia, ataxia-telangiectasia and Wiskott-Aldrich are classic inherited genomic-instability/immunodeficiency syndromes linked to haematological malignancies. Klinefelter is the odd member here.
Question 25

What is the normal approximate CD4+:CD8+ T-cell ratio?

  1. A. 2:1
  2. B. 1:2
  3. C. 1:1
  4. D. 2:2
  5. E. 1.2:1
AnswerA. Approximately 2:1
Why / how: CD4 helper T cells normally outnumber CD8 cytotoxic T cells; a ratio around 2:1 is a classic teaching value.
Question 26

Which is the odd member among the listed gastrointestinal/nutritional causes associated with vitamin B12 deficiency?

  1. A. Stagnant intestinal loop
  2. B. Ileal resection
  3. C. Crohn disease
  4. D. Fish tapeworm
  5. E. Tropical sprue
AnswerE. Tropical sprue — best exam answer
Why / how: The question is imperfect because tropical sprue can also cause B12 deficiency. The other options are more directly classic mechanisms: terminal ileal loss/disease, bacterial competition, or Diphyllobothrium latum competition.
Question 27

Which disorder is characterized by reduced mobilization/motility of neutrophils from bone marrow?

  1. A. Chediak-Higashi syndrome
  2. B. Lazy leukocyte syndrome
  3. C. Chronic granulomatous disease
  4. D. Myeloperoxidase deficiency
  5. E. Felty syndrome
AnswerB. Lazy leukocyte syndrome
Why / how: This rare disorder involves abnormal neutrophil motility/chemotaxis and impaired mobilization.
Question 28

Which is an immune-related cause of neutropenia?

  1. A. Chediak-Higashi syndrome
  2. B. Lazy leukocyte syndrome
  3. C. Chronic granulomatous disease
  4. D. Myeloperoxidase deficiency
  5. E. Felty syndrome
AnswerE. Felty syndrome
Why / how: Felty syndrome is classically rheumatoid arthritis + splenomegaly + neutropenia, with immune-mediated destruction and splenic sequestration contributing.
Question 29

What is the purpose of a major cross-match in transfusion medicine?

  1. A. Identify donor blood group
  2. B. Identify patient's blood group
  3. C. Merely 'give safe blood'
  4. D. Detect recipient antibodies directed against antigens on donor RBCs
  5. E. Prevent haemolytic disease of the newborn
AnswerD. Detect recipient antibodies against donor RBC antigens
Why / how: Major crossmatch mixes recipient serum/plasma with donor red cells. Agglutination or haemolysis indicates incompatibility.
Question 30

In transfusion medicine, what does forward blood grouping involve?

  1. A. Patient RBCs + anti-A and anti-B test sera
  2. B. Patient RBCs + exogenous antigens
  3. C. No agglutination reaction
  4. D. Patient serum + known A or B reagent RBCs
  5. E. Agglutination at room temperature only
AnswerA. Patient RBCs are tested with known anti-A and anti-B antisera
Why / how: Forward grouping asks which ABO antigens are present on the patient's red cells. Reverse grouping instead tests the patient's serum against known A and B reagent cells.

Aplastic anaemia, sickle cell disease & vitamin B12 — Q31–46

Question 31

Which virus is classically implicated in hepatitis-associated aplastic anaemia among the listed options?

  1. A. HIV
  2. B. HBV
  3. C. Parvovirus B19
  4. D. HPV
  5. E. EBV
AnswerB. HBV — best answer
Why / how: Hepatitis-associated aplastic anaemia is recognized. Parvovirus B19 more classically causes pure red-cell aplasia or transient aplastic crisis rather than generalized pancytopenic aplastic anaemia.
Question 32

What is the sickle-cell crisis in which large amounts of blood suddenly pool in the spleen?

  1. A. Aplastic crisis
  2. B. Sequestration crisis
  3. C. Vaso-occlusive crisis
  4. D. Haemolytic crisis
  5. E. Hyperhaemolytic crisis
AnswerB. Sequestration crisis
Why / how: Blood becomes trapped in the spleen, causing rapid splenic enlargement, sudden severe anaemia, reduced circulating volume and possible shock.
Question 33

Which organ/tissue is commonly affected by vitamin B12 deficiency among the listed options?

  1. A. Brain
  2. B. Gastrointestinal mucosa
  3. C. Bone
  4. D. Kidney
  5. E. Liver
AnswerB. Gastrointestinal mucosa
Why / how: B12 deficiency impairs DNA synthesis, so rapidly dividing tissues such as GI epithelium are strongly affected. Neurological tissue is also important clinically.
Question 34

Neural tube defects are most strongly associated with deficiency of which nutrient?

  1. A. Iron
  2. B. Vitamin B12
  3. C. Pernicious anaemia
  4. D. Folate
  5. E. Vitamin B6
AnswerD. Folate
Why / how: Folate is crucial for DNA synthesis and early neural-tube closure. Deficiency increases the risk of spina bifida, anencephaly and related defects.
Question 35

Which genotype is the classic definition of sickle-cell anaemia in this exam?

  1. A. AS
  2. B. AC
  3. C. SS
  4. D. SC
  5. E. S/β-thalassaemia
AnswerC. SS
Why / how: HbSS is classic sickle-cell anaemia. Strictly, sickle-cell disease is broader and also includes HbSC and HbS/β-thalassaemia.
Question 36

What is the approximate concentration of HbA₂ in a neonate?

  1. A. 24%
  2. B. 2.5%
  3. C. 0.5%
  4. D. 97%
  5. E. 12%
AnswerC. About 0.5%
Why / how: HbA₂ is α₂δ₂. Delta-chain production is low at birth, so HbA₂ is very low in neonates and rises later toward adult levels.
Question 37

Which vitamin B12-related transport protein is synthesized in the stomach?

  1. A. Transcobalamin
  2. B. Intrinsic factor
  3. C. Cobalamin
  4. D. Cobalophilin
  5. E. Globulin
AnswerB. Intrinsic factor
Why / how: Intrinsic factor is secreted by gastric parietal cells and is required for receptor-mediated B12 absorption in the terminal ileum.
Question 38

Which parasite is the best available option for causing chronic blood loss leading to iron-deficiency anaemia?

  1. A. Plasmodium
  2. B. Fish tapeworm
  3. C. Schistosoma haematobium
  4. D. Liver fluke
  5. E. None
AnswerC. Schistosoma haematobium — best available option
Why / how: Hookworm is the classic parasite causing iron-deficiency anaemia but is not listed. S. haematobium can cause chronic urinary blood loss and iron deficiency.
Question 39

Which is NOT primarily an intestinal cause of vitamin B12 deficiency?

  1. A. Ileal resection
  2. B. Crohn disease
  3. C. Pernicious anaemia
  4. D. Fish tapeworm
  5. E. Stagnant intestinal loop
AnswerC. Pernicious anaemia
Why / how: Pernicious anaemia begins in the stomach: autoimmune destruction of parietal cells causes intrinsic-factor deficiency. The other options act primarily through intestinal malabsorption or competition.
Question 40

Where is vitamin B12 absorbed?

  1. A. Jejunum
  2. B. Ileum
  3. C. Duodenum
  4. D. Stomach
  5. E. Colon
AnswerB. Ileum — specifically terminal ileum
Why / how: The intrinsic-factor–B12 complex binds receptors in the terminal ileum for absorption.
Question 41

Which protein first binds food-derived vitamin B12 in the upper gastrointestinal tract?

  1. A. Transcobalamin
  2. B. Intrinsic factor
  3. C. Cobalamin
  4. D. Cobalophilin / haptocorrin
  5. E. Albumin
AnswerD. Cobalophilin / haptocorrin
Why / how: B12 first binds haptocorrin in saliva/stomach. Pancreatic enzymes later digest haptocorrin in the duodenum, allowing B12 to bind intrinsic factor.
Question 42

Where does oral iron absorption occur maximally?

  1. A. Stomach
  2. B. Jejunum
  3. C. First part of the duodenum
  4. D. Ileum
  5. E. Colon
AnswerC. First part of the duodenum
Why / how: Iron is absorbed mainly in the duodenum and proximal jejunum, with the duodenum being the best answer.
Question 43

Approximately what percentage of oral iron is absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract under ordinary conditions?

  1. A. 5–10%
  2. B. 1–3%
  3. C. 35–40%
  4. D. 15–25%
  5. E. 0.5%
AnswerA. About 5–10%
Why / how: Only a minority of ingested iron is normally absorbed. Absorption rises when iron stores are low and falls when hepcidin is high.
Question 44

Which red-cell index is most commonly used for the morphological classification of anaemia?

  1. A. Hb
  2. B. MCH
  3. C. MCHC
  4. D. MCV
  5. E. RDW
AnswerD. MCV

Question 45

What is the first site of fetal haematopoiesis?

  2. B. Thymus
  3. C. Yolk sac
  4. D. Kidney
  5. E. Skull
AnswerC. Yolk sac

Question 46

Which hormone mediates the response to tissue hypoxia by stimulating erythropoiesis?

  2. B. Haemoglobin
  3. C. Erythropoietin

Figure-based: multiple myeloma — Q54–57

Question 54

In the figure-based case suggestive of multiple myeloma, which renal pathology may result?

Question 55

Which clinical feature is likely in multiple myeloma with osteolytic bone disease?

  2. B. Hyperreflexia
  3. C. Splenic rupture
  4. D. Haemarthrosis
  5. E. Pathological fracture
AnswerE. Pathological fracture
Why / how: Myeloma drives osteoclast activation and suppresses normal bone formation, causing punched-out osteolytic lesions, bone pain and fractures.
Question 56

Which demographic option best fits the epidemiology of multiple myeloma among the listed choices?

  1. A. Young White men
  2. B. Children
  3. C. Young Black women
  4. D. Elderly White women
  5. E. Elderly Black women
AnswerE. Elderly Black women — best available option
Why / how: Multiple myeloma is primarily a disease of older adults and occurs at higher incidence in people of African ancestry. Men overall are somewhat more commonly affected than women, so the option is imperfect.
Question 57

What happens to ESR in multiple myeloma?

  1. A. Low
  2. B. Normal
  3. C. Moderately low
  4. D. High
  5. E. Unchanged

Figure-based: Hodgkin lymphoma — Q58–61

  5. E. None

Why / how: Old FAB L2 morphology describes larger, more heterogeneous lymphoblasts than L1. Modern classification relies much more on immunophenotype and molecular genetics.

Question 67

The laboratory data show a very high platelet count in a 23-year-old pregnant woman. What is the most likely haematological abnormality among the listed options?

  2. B. Thrombophilia
  3. C. Spherocytosis

AnswerB. Thrombophilia — intended exam answer
Why / how: Strictly, the laboratory abnormality is thrombocytosis, not 'thrombophilia'. Thrombophilia means a tendency to thrombosis. The option is poorly worded, but B is clearly the intended answer.
Question 68

This patient with marked thrombocytosis may present with which complication?

  1. A. DIC
  2. B. Thrombosis
  3. C. Bleeding diathesis only
  4. D. Epistaxis only
  5. E. Haemarthrosis
AnswerB. Thrombosis
Why / how: Marked thrombocytosis can increase arterial, venous and microvascular thrombosis risk. Extremely high counts can paradoxically also cause bleeding through acquired von Willebrand dysfunction.
Question 69

Which test is the most appropriate from the listed traditional options for assessing the platelet-related primary haemostatic problem?

  2. B. PT
  3. C. Bleeding time
  4. D. Factor assay
  5. E. D-dimer
AnswerC. Bleeding time — intended older exam answer
Why / how: PT and aPTT mainly assess coagulation-factor pathways. Bleeding time was historically used to assess primary haemostasis/platelet function, although modern practice uses more specific platelet and von Willebrand testing.
